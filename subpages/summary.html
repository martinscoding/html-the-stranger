Summary

Albert Camus’ novel The Stranger, first published in 1942, tells the story of Meursault, a man who seems strangely detached from the world around him. The book begins with his mother’s death, which he acknowledges with little visible emotion. This reaction shocks others and immediately sets him apart as someone who does not behave the way society expects.

After the funeral, Meursault continues his ordinary life in Algiers. He starts spending time with Marie, a former co-worker, and becomes involved in the problems of his neighbor Raymond. One summer day, these events build up to a confrontation on the beach. Overcome by the oppressive heat and blinding sunlight, Meursault shoots and kills an Arab man. The act is sudden, almost as if it happens outside of his own control, and it changes the course of his life.

The second half of the novel focuses on Meursault’s trial. Rather than carefully examining the murder itself, the court becomes obsessed with his character. His indifference at his mother’s funeral is treated as proof that he is heartless and dangerous. In the end, he is sentenced to death not only for the crime he committed, but also for failing to live up to society’s expectations of grief, love, and morality.

Facing execution, Meursault finally confronts life on its own terms. He realizes that the world is indifferent to human struggles and that searching for ultimate meaning is pointless. Yet this realization gives him peace: if life has no higher purpose, then he is free to accept it as it is. Camus uses Meursault’s story to show how one can embrace the “absurd” condition of existence and still find a way to live authentically.
