Philosophical Analysis

One of the reasons The Stranger is such a powerful novel is because it embodies Albert Camus’ philosophy of the absurd. Camus argued that life has no built-in meaning. We search for purpose, but the universe remains silent. This clash between our desire for meaning and the world’s indifference is what he called “the absurd.”

Meursault, the main character, illustrates this idea through his actions and attitudes. His lack of emotion at his mother’s funeral and his indifference toward love, work, and ambition disturb those around him. But Camus doesn’t portray him as a monster. Instead, Meursault is honest in a way that most people are not: he refuses to pretend that life has more meaning than it really does.

The scene on the beach, where Meursault kills the Arab man, can be read as the height of the absurd. The act is not carefully planned or driven by hatred—it happens under the crushing heat of the sun, almost as if nature itself pushes him to pull the trigger. It shows how fragile our sense of control really is, and how quickly life can shift into chaos without reason.

Paradoxically, Meursault’s time in prison becomes the moment when he reaches clarity. Knowing that he will be executed, he understands that death is the one certainty all people share. By accepting this truth, he no longer seeks comfort in illusions. Instead, he learns to value life itself, brief and fragile as it is. In this acceptance, Camus suggests, lies true freedom.

The novel therefore challenges us as readers: if the universe is indifferent, how should we live? For Camus, the answer is not despair but courage. Like Meursault, we can embrace life without appeal to higher meaning, finding authenticity and even joy in the face of the absurd.
